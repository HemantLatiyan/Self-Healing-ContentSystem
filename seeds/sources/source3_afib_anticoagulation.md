# Atrial Fibrillation: Anticoagulation Quick Reference

This reference summarizes current recommendations for stroke prevention in non-valvular atrial fibrillation (AF). It is intended as a quick decision aid for clinicians and does not replace full clinical judgment.

## Stroke Risk Stratification

Stroke risk in non-valvular AF is estimated with the CHA2DS2-VASc score. Anticoagulation is recommended at a score of 2 or higher in men and 3 or higher in women. A score of 1 in men or 2 in women is a discussion point: anticoagulation may be offered after shared decision making.

The HAS-BLED score should be calculated in parallel to estimate bleeding risk, but a high HAS-BLED score is not by itself a reason to withhold anticoagulation. It should instead trigger modification of reversible bleeding risk factors.

## Choice of Anticoagulant

For most patients with non-valvular AF, a direct oral anticoagulant (DOAC) is preferred over warfarin. The four DOACs available are apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, and edoxaban. DOACs offer comparable or superior stroke prevention with lower rates of intracranial hemorrhage and do not require routine INR monitoring.

Warfarin remains the agent of choice for patients with mechanical heart valves, moderate to severe mitral stenosis, antiphospholipid syndrome, or end stage renal disease where DOAC data are limited.

## Apixaban Dose Reduction

Apixaban is dosed at 5 mg twice daily in most patients. The reduced dose of 2.5 mg twice daily is indicated when the patient meets at least two of the following three criteria: age 80 years or older, body weight 60 kg or less, or serum creatinine 1.5 mg/dL or greater.

## Renal Dosing

DOAC selection in chronic kidney disease depends on creatinine clearance.

- Apixaban: usable across most CKD stages, with dose reduction as above.
- Rivaroxaban: 15 mg once daily for CrCl 15 to 50 mL/min; avoid below 15.
- Dabigatran: 150 mg twice daily for CrCl above 30 mL/min; 75 mg twice daily for CrCl 15 to 30 (US only); avoid below 15.
- Edoxaban: 30 mg once daily for CrCl 15 to 50 mL/min; avoid above 95 or below 15.

## Reversal Agents

Specific reversal agents are available for life threatening bleeding or before urgent surgery.

- Dabigatran is reversed with idarucizumab (5 g IV in two divided doses).
- Apixaban and rivaroxaban are reversed with andexanet alfa, dosed based on the agent, the dose taken, and time since last dose.

Four factor prothrombin complex concentrate (4F-PCC) is an off label alternative when andexanet alfa is unavailable.

## Antithrombotic Therapy After PCI

**Guideline change (2025 revision):** Routine triple therapy is no longer recommended for most patients with AF undergoing PCI. The accumulated evidence from the AUGUSTUS, ENTRUST-AF PCI, and PIONEER AF-PCI trials demonstrates that omitting aspirin after the periprocedural window substantially reduces major bleeding without a meaningful increase in stent thrombosis.

Current practice for most patients with AF undergoing elective or non-complex PCI is dual antithrombotic therapy from day one after the procedure: a DOAC at the stroke prevention dose plus a P2Y12 inhibitor. Aspirin is given only during the periprocedural admission and discontinued at discharge. Dual therapy continues for 6 to 12 months, after which the P2Y12 inhibitor is also stopped and the patient remains on DOAC monotherapy.

Triple therapy beyond the periprocedural admission should now be reserved for the subset of patients at very high ischemic risk and acceptable bleeding risk, such as those with prior stent thrombosis, complex multi-vessel disease, or recent acute coronary syndrome with large thrombus burden.

Clopidogrel remains the preferred P2Y12 inhibitor in this setting because of its lower bleeding risk relative to ticagrelor or prasugrel.

## Periprocedural Management

For procedures with low bleeding risk, DOACs can often be continued. For higher bleeding risk procedures, DOACs are typically held for 24 to 48 hours preoperatively, with longer holds in renal impairment. Bridging with low molecular weight heparin is not routinely recommended for DOAC users.
