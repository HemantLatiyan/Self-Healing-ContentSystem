# U.S. Retirement Account Contribution Limits and Rules

This reference summarizes the contribution limits and key rules for the most common U.S. tax advantaged retirement accounts. Figures reflect the limits in effect for the 2026 tax year.

## 401(k), 403(b), and Most 457(b) Plans

The IRS elective deferral limit for employee contributions to a 401(k), 403(b), or governmental 457(b) plan is $24,000 in 2026. This limit applies in aggregate across all 401(k) and 403(b) plans an employee participates in during the year; the 457(b) limit is separate and may be stacked.

Employees who are age 50 or older by year end may make an additional catch-up contribution of $7,500, bringing the standard total elective deferral to $31,500. Employees who are age 60 through 63 may make an enhanced catch-up contribution of $11,250 under the SECURE 2.0 super catch-up provision.

The overall annual additions limit, which includes employee deferrals, employer match, and after tax contributions, is $72,000 in 2026 (or $79,500 including the standard catch-up). This is the cap relevant to the mega backdoor Roth strategy.

## IRAs

The annual contribution limit for a traditional or Roth IRA is $7,500 in 2026. The catch-up contribution for individuals age 50 or older is $1,100, bringing the total to $8,600.

Roth IRA contributions phase out based on modified adjusted gross income. For single filers in 2026 the phase out begins at $153,000 and ends at $168,000. For married filing jointly the phase out begins at $242,000 and ends at $252,000.

The backdoor Roth IRA strategy, which involves a non deductible traditional IRA contribution followed by a Roth conversion, remains legal under current statute despite recurring legislative proposals to close it. The pro rata rule applies to conversions and can create unexpected tax consequences for individuals with pre tax IRA balances.

## Catch-Up Contributions

**Major rule change in effect for 2026.** Section 603 of the SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022, after a two year administrative delay under IRS Notice 2023-62, took effect January 1, 2026. Catch-up contributions to a 401(k), 403(b), or governmental 457(b) plan must now be made on a Roth (after tax) basis for any employee whose prior year FICA wages from the sponsoring employer exceeded $145,000 (indexed; $150,000 for the 2026 determination year).

The practical consequences:

- Affected high earners no longer receive a current year tax deduction on their catch-up contributions.
- Plans that do not offer a Roth feature must either add one or prohibit catch-up contributions entirely for affected employees.
- The mandate applies only to the catch-up amount, not to the underlying elective deferral.
- Wages are measured per employer, so a switch of employers mid year can change the determination for the following year.

Employees whose prior year wages were at or below the $145,000 threshold continue to make catch-up contributions on either a pre tax or Roth basis at the participant's discretion. Self employed individuals and partners receiving guaranteed payments rather than W-2 wages are outside the scope of the mandate.

## Required Minimum Distributions

The SECURE Act of 2019 raised the RMD beginning age from 70.5 to 72, and the SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022 raised it further to 73 effective January 1, 2023. The RMD age is scheduled to rise again to 75 in 2033.

The penalty for a missed RMD was reduced by SECURE 2.0 from 50 percent of the shortfall to 25 percent, and to 10 percent if corrected within a defined correction window.

Roth IRAs are not subject to RMDs during the original owner's lifetime. As of the 2024 tax year, designated Roth accounts inside an employer plan (Roth 401(k), Roth 403(b)) are also no longer subject to RMDs during the participant's lifetime, aligning their treatment with Roth IRAs.

## Traditional vs Roth Decision

The traditional vs Roth decision depends on the participant's expected marginal tax rate in retirement compared to today.

- Choose **traditional** if the expected retirement marginal rate is meaningfully lower than the current marginal rate. Most high earners in their peak earning years are in this category, subject to the Roth catch-up mandate above.
- Choose **Roth** if the expected retirement marginal rate is similar to or higher than the current rate, if the participant wants tax diversification, or if the participant wants to avoid RMDs.

A blended approach is common: contribute pre tax up to the deferral limit, then use a Roth IRA or after tax 401(k) with in plan Roth conversion for additional Roth exposure.
