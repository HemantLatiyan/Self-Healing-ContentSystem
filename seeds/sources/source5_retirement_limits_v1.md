# U.S. Retirement Account Contribution Limits and Rules

This reference summarizes the contribution limits and key rules for the most common U.S. tax advantaged retirement accounts. Figures reflect the limits in effect for the 2024 tax year.

## 401(k), 403(b), and Most 457(b) Plans

The IRS elective deferral limit for employee contributions to a 401(k), 403(b), or governmental 457(b) plan is $23,000 in 2024. This limit applies in aggregate across all 401(k) and 403(b) plans an employee participates in during the year; the 457(b) limit is separate and may be stacked.

Employees who are age 50 or older by year end may make an additional catch-up contribution of $7,500, bringing the total elective deferral to $30,500.

The overall annual additions limit, which includes employee deferrals, employer match, and after tax contributions, is $69,000 in 2024 (or $76,500 including catch-up). This is the cap relevant to the mega backdoor Roth strategy.

## IRAs

The annual contribution limit for a traditional or Roth IRA is $7,000 in 2024. The catch-up contribution for individuals age 50 or older is $1,000, bringing the total to $8,000.

Roth IRA contributions phase out based on modified adjusted gross income. For single filers in 2024 the phase out begins at $146,000 and ends at $161,000. For married filing jointly the phase out begins at $230,000 and ends at $240,000.

The backdoor Roth IRA strategy, which involves a non deductible traditional IRA contribution followed by a Roth conversion, remains legal under current statute despite recurring legislative proposals to close it. The pro rata rule applies to conversions and can create unexpected tax consequences for individuals with pre tax IRA balances.

## Catch-Up Contributions

All catch-up contributions to 401(k), 403(b), and 457(b) plans are currently made on a pre tax basis by default. Plan participants may choose to designate them as Roth (after tax) if the plan supports a Roth feature, but this is optional.

Section 603 of the SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022 contains a provision that would mandate Roth treatment of catch-up contributions for employees whose prior year Social Security wages exceeded $145,000 (indexed). This provision was originally scheduled to take effect in 2024 but was delayed by IRS Notice 2023-62 to provide an administrative transition period. The current effective date is January 1, 2026, after which the high earner mandate will apply to all affected catch-up contributions.

For 2024, all catch-up contributions remain electively pre tax or Roth at the participant's discretion, regardless of income.

## Required Minimum Distributions

The SECURE Act of 2019 raised the RMD beginning age from 70.5 to 72, and the SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022 raised it further to 73 effective January 1, 2023. The RMD age is scheduled to rise again to 75 in 2033.

The penalty for a missed RMD was reduced by SECURE 2.0 from 50 percent of the shortfall to 25 percent, and to 10 percent if corrected within a defined correction window.

Roth IRAs are not subject to RMDs during the original owner's lifetime. Beginning in 2024, designated Roth accounts inside an employer plan (Roth 401(k), Roth 403(b)) are also no longer subject to RMDs during the participant's lifetime, aligning their treatment with Roth IRAs.

## Traditional vs Roth Decision

The traditional vs Roth decision depends on the participant's expected marginal tax rate in retirement compared to today.

- Choose **traditional** if the expected retirement marginal rate is meaningfully lower than the current marginal rate. Most high earners in their peak earning years are in this category.
- Choose **Roth** if the expected retirement marginal rate is similar to or higher than the current rate, if the participant wants tax diversification, or if the participant wants to avoid RMDs.

A blended approach is common: contribute pre tax up to the deferral limit, then use a Roth IRA or after tax 401(k) with in plan Roth conversion for additional Roth exposure.
